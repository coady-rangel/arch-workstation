# Engineering Principles

---

## Purpose

This document defines the engineering principles that guide every decision in this project.

Architectures may evolve.

Roadmaps will change.

Features will come and go.

These principles should remain relatively stable and serve as the foundation for every future decision.

---

# Core Principles

## 1. Invest in systems that reduce the cost of future work.

We intentionally invest time in architecture, automation, validation, documentation, and governance because they compound over the lifetime of the project.

The goal is not to make today's work easier.

The goal is to make every future change easier.

---

## 2. Design systems before designing features.

Individual features solve immediate problems.

Systems enable solving future problems efficiently.

Whenever possible, improve the system before expanding functionality.

---

## 3. Treat projects as living systems.

A project is never "finished."

It evolves.

Documentation, architecture, and tooling should evolve alongside implementation.

---

## 4. Optimize for maintainability.

Future maintainability is more valuable than short-term convenience.

Prefer solutions that are:

- understandable
- reproducible
- testable
- extensible

---

## 5. Validate continuously.

Implementation is incomplete until it has been validated.

Testing, verification, and documentation are part of engineering—not optional follow-up work.

---

## 6. Document the why.

Code explains how.

Documentation explains why.

Future contributors should understand not only what exists, but the reasoning behind it.

---

## 7. Prefer clarity over cleverness.

Readable systems outlive clever systems.

Simple solutions are easier to debug, extend, and teach.

---

## 8. Small improvements compound.

Large projects are built through consistent incremental progress.

Each improvement should leave the project in a better state than it was found.

---

## 9. Build with future engineers in mind.

Write code and documentation assuming someone else will maintain it.

That future engineer may be yourself six months from now.

---

## 10. Engineering is an investment.

Time spent improving process, tooling, documentation, and architecture is not overhead.

It is an investment that pays dividends throughout the life of the project.
